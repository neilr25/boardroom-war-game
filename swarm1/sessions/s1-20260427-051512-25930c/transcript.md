## 💻 CTO

This isn't an MVP; it's a hallucination. You’re pitching a "Health Flywheel," but you've described a hardware nightmare.

**The Brutal Reality:**
You are attempting to solve three distinct, high-risk engineering problems simultaneously: precision hardware, edge computer vision (CV), and medical-grade biometric telemetry. 

1.  **The "AI" is a gimmick:** Analyzing "chewing patterns" via a doorbell-camera-grade lens is a science project. To get actionable data on gait or mastication, you need high-frame-rate, multi-angle sensors and a sterile environment. Cats are chaotic; they will knock the sensor out of alignment or cover the lens in grime. Your "proprietary dataset" will be 90% noise.
2.  **The Hardware Trap:** Hardware doesn't scale like software. "Scaling the first production run" is where seed money goes to die. Supply chain volatility, firmware bugs, and physical failure rates (cats are destructive) will eat your runway before you ship 1,000 units.
3.  **The Liability Gap:** "Automatically adjusting caloric intake" based on an unproven AI model is a lawsuit waiting to happen. If your AI miscalculates and starves a cat or overfeeds a diabetic one, your "narrative" won't save you.

**The 6-Week Pivot (The only way this survives):**
Stop pretending you're a biotech company. Build a **dumb feeder with a smart app**.
*   **Week 1-3:** Basic dispensing hardware + weight scale (load cell).
*   **Week 4-6:** Simple telemetry (Did the cat eat? How much?).
*   **The Scale Path:** Once you prove people will actually pay for a subscription to track weight, *then* you iterate on the CV.

**Verdict:** This is a science project wrapped in a VC pitch deck. Cut the "predictive longevity" hype and build a product that actually dispenses kibble without crashing.

## 🛒 Customer

As a Procurement Director, I don’t buy "flywheels" or "narratives." I buy solutions that solve a quantified problem. The CEO is selling a vision; I’m looking at the P&L and the risk register.

**The Reality Check:**
This is a classic case of "solution looking for a problem." The "analog" nature of feeding isn't a pain point for the consumer—it's a convenience. Adding high-cost sensors to a plastic hopper creates a massive price premium for a marginal utility.

**Switching Costs:**
Low to Moderate. If the subscription is too high or the hardware fails, the customer reverts to a $20 plastic bowl. There is no "lock-in" here unless the data is integrated into a mandatory insurance policy.

**Willingness to Pay:**
Low. Pet owners spend on *outcomes* (health), not *inputs* (feeders). I suspect a ceiling on hardware pricing. If the ROI is "predictive longevity," the CEO needs to prove that this device actually reduces vet bills by X% over Y years. Without that data, it's a luxury gadget, not a health tool.

**Top 3 Objections:**
1. **Hardware Fragility vs. Environment:** Cats are destructive. Sensors are delicate. What is the replacement cycle? If I’m buying this for a fleet or a corporate wellness perk, the maintenance cost will kill the ROI.
2. **Data Privacy & Over-Engineering:** Why do I need computer vision to tell me my cat isn't eating? A simple weight-sensor in the bowl does that for 1/10th the cost. This is "AI-native" bloat.
3. **Pricing Opacity:** Is this a one-time CAPEX or a perpetual OPEX (subscription)? If the "health flywheel" requires a monthly fee to access the data I already paid for in the hardware, the churn will be astronomical.

## ⚖️ Counsel

This is a classic "narrative" play masking a regulatory minefield. The CEO is selling a "Health Flywheel"; I see a liability spiral.

**The Deal-Killer: The "Medical Device" Pivot**
The moment the CEO claims the AI "detects a decrease in appetite" to "trigger a telehealth consultation" and "automatically adjusts caloric intake," he has crossed the line from a consumer appliance to a **Class II Medical Device**. If this device makes autonomous diagnostic or prescriptive decisions (adjusting calories based on biometric data), the FDA will treat it as a medical device. Without 510(k) clearance, the company is selling an unapproved medical device. That is a shut-down event.

**The Regulatory & Privacy Thickets:**
*   **GDPR/CCPA:** He’s collecting "biometric sensors" and "computer vision" data. While the cat isn't a citizen, the *owner* is. Cameras in the home are high-risk. If the AI captures humans in the background or maps the home interior, we have a massive privacy exposure.
*   **Telehealth Liability:** Integrating telehealth triggers creates a "referral" or "practitioner" nexus. If the AI misses a kidney failure signal and the user relies on the "predictive longevity" promise, we are looking at massive professional negligence/product liability suits.

**IP & Patent Landscape:**
The "behavioral dataset" is a fantasy. Computer vision for pet weight/gait is a crowded field. I suspect a "patent thicket" from incumbents (Mars, Nestle) or academic labs. If we don't have a "freedom to operate" (FTO) analysis, we are just funding a lawsuit.

**Verdict:** I’m not writing a check until the CEO defines the "Health Flywheel" as a *wellness* tool, not a *diagnostic* one, and provides a full FTO on the CV sensors. **Hard Pass until the risk is ring-fenced.**
